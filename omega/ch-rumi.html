Jalāl al-Dīn Rūmī — Mevlana, "our master" — was born in Balkh (present-day Afghanistan) in 1207, traveled westward as a child fleeing the Mongol invasions, and settled in Konya in Anatolia, where he lived and taught until his death in 1273. He is the author of the Masnavi — six books of Persian verse, approximately 25,000 couplets — and the Divan-e Shams-e Tabrizi, the lyric collection dedicated to his mystical friend Shams of Tabriz. He is the most widely read poet in the world today in translated form.

He is also the person who made the contact manifold into a liturgy.

The Sama and the Contact Form

The sama — the Sufi listening ceremony — is the ritual practice of the Mevlevi order that Rumi's son Sultan Walad formalized after Rumi's death. In its most visible form, it involves the sema whirling: the dervish rotates on the left foot, right arm raised (palm up, receiving from the heavens), left arm lowered (palm down, transmitting to the earth). The rotation is sustained for extended periods — sometimes an hour or more — in a state of deep meditative absorption.

Fana: The Dissolution into Union

Rumi's central mystical concept is fana — annihilation, dissolution. The Sufi practitioner in deep sama does not merely rotate. They dissolve: the individual ego is, temporarily, no longer the subject of experience. The rotation continues, the body functions, but the "I" that normally claims the experience has stepped back. Rumi's theology of fana is the U operator taken to its logical limit: the union is total. The individual subsystem does not merely join a larger system — it temporarily ceases to be a separately identified subsystem. $\tau_{12} \leq \min(\tau_1, \tau_2)$: the threshold of the union is determined by the lower threshold. Fana is the case $\tau_2 \to \infty$: the mystic's threshold approaches the divine threshold, which is infinite (or rather, $\tau = 2$ approached but never reached from below).

In the Theorem of Union (Def 3.1 of GTCT), two systems join into a larger system with $\tau_{12} \leq \min(\tau_1, \tau_2)$. The Reed Flute

The Masnavi opens with the image of the reed flute — ney — cut from the reed bed and crying for its origin. This is the C operator as cosmology: separation from the source is the precondition of music. The flute makes sound only because it has been cut, hollowed, and separated. The separation is not a tragedy to be overcome — it is the mechanism by which the system becomes capable of resonance. The reed bed is the source. The cut is C. The sound is K. The reunion is U. The seeking of the reed for its origin is the Omega Point — $\tau = 2$, the limit the ladder approaches without reaching it in any finite song.

Listen to this reed how it complains, telling tales of separations. Since they cut me from the reed bed, man and woman have lamented with me.

— Rumi, Masnavi Book I, opening (Coleman Barks translation)
