Part 1 · the construction

Beating the grid — unit distances

Erdős's 1946 question: place $n$ points in the plane; how many pairs can be exactly distance $1$ apart? For nearly eighty years the square grid was believed essentially optimal. In May 2026 an OpenAI reasoning model disproved that, exhibiting an infinite family of constructions — built from algebraic number theory — that achieve $n^{1+\delta}$ unit-distance pairs, a genuine polynomial improvement. Will Sawin (Princeton) refined the exponent to $\delta = 0.014$. The argument was checked by Timothy Gowers, Noga Alon, and others; formal journal peer review was still in progress.

Its epistemic status

This is a real advance — publishable and celebrated even if a human had done it. But its truth rests on expert human checking, not machine verification. It has not been formalized, and formalizing this particular construction in Lean would itself be a substantial research project (the number-theoretic machinery is deep). "Solved," here, is the classical sense: trusted because the right people read it and believe it.

Part 2 · the formalization

Kernel-checked — Erdős #728

The other 2026 result is the one this volume actually cares about, because its output is a proof a machine can certify. Erdős Problem #728 concerns a delicate factorial-divisibility statement: for any constants $0 < C_1 < C_2$ and $0 < \varepsilon < \tfrac12$, there are infinitely many triples $(a,b,n)$ with $\varepsilon n \le a,b \le (1-\varepsilon)n$ such that $a!\,b! \mid n!\,(a+b-n)!$ and $C_1 \log n < a+b-n < C_2 \log n$.

It was resolved by a pipeline of GPT-5.2 Pro (OpenAI) and Aristotle (Harmonic), operated by Kevin Barreto, and announced on 4 January 2026 — reported as the first Erdős problem regarded as fully resolved autonomously by an AI system. Crucially, the deliverable was not prose but a Lean proof. The strategy routes through Kummer's theorem — which turns the $p$-adic valuation of a binomial-type quantity into a count of carries in base-$p$ addition — and then a counting argument that finds integers whose base-$p$ expansions are "carry-rich but spike-free," forcing the required divisibility for every prime $p \le 2k$.

The statement, rendered illustratively in Lean (this is a paraphrase of the theorem, not Aristotle's proof — see arXiv:2601.07421 for the real artifact):

Why this one belongs in The Seed

Part 1 is trusted because Gowers read it. Part 2 is trusted because the kernel read it — the same standard this volume holds itself to (0 axioms beyond Mathlib, 0 sorry). The frontier worth marking isn't "AI does mathematics"; it is "AI produces proofs a kernel can certify." #728 is the first Erdős problem to cross that line, and it is the machine analogue of the series proving itself.

The collaborator

A new node in the Erdős graph

Erdős measured mathematics by collaboration — the famous graph of who has written with whom. What 2026 adds is a new kind of node: a machine that does not merely assist but returns a certified proof. It is fitting that the first such node attaches to an Erdős problem. The man who dreamed of "The Book" — the transfinite volume of the most perfect proofs — now has proofs written into a book a machine can read and check line by line. The portrait of the man is in Vol VII · Erdős; this chapter is what became of his problems when the collaborator stopped being human.
