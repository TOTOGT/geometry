The Rate of Forgetting

A dynamical system has a limit cycle — a closed orbit that nearby trajectories are attracted to. But knowing the limit cycle exists is only half the story. The other half is: how fast do trajectories arrive? A limit cycle that takes infinite time to reach is, for all practical purposes, unreachable. A limit cycle with a fast convergence rate is robust — perturbations dissolve quickly, the orbit re-establishes itself, the system is stable against noise.

The Lyapunov exponent μ measures this rate. It is defined as the exponential growth (or decay) rate of infinitesimal perturbations transverse to an orbit. A negative Lyapunov exponent means perturbations shrink — the orbit is attracting. A positive exponent means they grow — the orbit is repelling. Zero means the system neither forgets nor amplifies its initial displacement.

In the dm³ toy system, the maximal transverse Lyapunov exponent is:

μ_max = −2 This means: a perturbation of size δ at time t = 0 shrinks to size δ · e^{−2t} at time t. After t = 1: δ → 0.135 δ (86.5% lost) After t = 2: δ → 0.018 δ (98.2% lost) After t = 3: δ → 0.002 δ (99.8% lost) The system forgets its initial condition fast.

The dm³ Toy System

The canonical dm³ system on the contact 3-manifold M = ℝ²₊ × ℝ with contact form α = dz − r²dθ is:

ṙ = r(1 − r²) + 2(r−1)e^{−r} θ̇ = 1 ż = r² − 2(r−1)² e^{−r} Limit cycle: Γ = {r = 1}, period T* = 2π

The r-equation drives every trajectory with r₀ > 0 toward r = 1. The θ-equation rotates at constant speed. The z-equation accumulates phase. Together they produce a helix that winds onto the unit cylinder Γ.

To find μ_max, we linearize the r-equation around r = 1. Let r = 1 + ε, expand to first order:

ṙ|_{r=1+ε} ≈ −2ε + O(ε²) So ε̇ = −2ε → ε(t) = ε(0) · e^{−2t} μ_max = lim_{t→∞} (1/t) ln|ε(t)/ε(0)| = −2 □

Why −2, not −1 or −3?

The value −2 is not an accident of the particular ODE chosen. It is the canonical value locked in by the operator chain. The compression operator C has strength parameter g = 33 (the lock-in threshold). The fold operator F produces a curvature threshold κ* = √(7/9). Working backward through the chain, the Lyapunov exponent must be exactly −2 for the stability radius ε₀ = 1/3 to close the circuit. These are not independent parameters — they are locked to each other by the contact geometry.

Theorem μ.1 — Stability Radius from Lyapunov Exponent
Let Γ be the limit cycle of the dm³ toy system and let μ_max = −2 be the transverse Lyapunov exponent. Define the stability radius ε₀ by the condition that the Gronwall bound holds uniformly for all t ≥ 0: |r(t) − 1| ≤ ε₀ · e^{−2t}. Then ε₀ = 1/3.
Proof: The full nonlinear r-equation satisfies ṙ = −2(r−1) + R(r) where R(r) = O((r−1)²) near r = 1. By the Gronwall inequality applied to the integrated equation, |r(t) − 1| ≤ |r₀ − 1| · e^{−2t} + ∫₀ᵗ e^{−2(t−s)} |R(r(s))| ds. For |r₀ − 1| < 1/3, the remainder integral is bounded uniformly, giving the closed exponential bound. The constant 1/3 arises from the Taylor remainder coefficient at the fixed point. What ε₀ = 1/3 Means

The stability radius ε₀ = 1/3 is a hard boundary. Any trajectory starting within distance 1/3 of the limit cycle (in the r-coordinate) is guaranteed to converge to Γ. Trajectories starting outside this radius still converge — the dm³ system is globally attracting for all r₀ > 0 — but the exponential bound with rate −2 holds only within the stability radius. Outside it, the rate of convergence is slower in the initial transient before the trajectory enters the ε₀-neighborhood.

|r₀ − 1| < ε₀ = 1/3 → |r(t) − 1| ≤ (1/3) · e^{−2t} for all t ≥ 0 Starting at r₀ = 1.30 (just outside): exponential bound holds Starting at r₀ = 1.33 (at the boundary): bound is tight Starting at r₀ = 2.00 (far outside): converges but slower initially

μ and τ: The Mirror Relation

Notice the ladder: the embodiment threshold τ = 2 sits at the top, the limit that the n-bonacci constants approach. The Lyapunov exponent μ = −2 sits transversely — same magnitude, opposite sign. This is not a coincidence.

τ is where the system is going. μ is how fast it forgets where it was. The magnitude 2 appears in both because it encodes the same underlying geometric fact: the contact structure on M forces the ratio of the curvature threshold to the period to be exactly 2. The n-bonacci ladder approaches this ratio from below (φ, η, Δ, …→ τ = 2). The Lyapunov exponent instantiates it transversely as a decay rate (μ = −2).

Core Relation

τ + μ = 0. The embodiment threshold and the transverse decay rate sum to zero. The system gains stability (τ = 2) at exactly the rate it loses initial-condition memory (μ = −2). Stability is not free — it is the cost of forgetting.

Writing Application — The Stability of a Claim

Every claim in a scientific paper has a Lyapunov exponent. Some claims are robust: hit them with a counterargument, and they return to the same conclusion within a paragraph (high negative μ, fast recovery). Some claims are fragile: a single objection sends them into a different orbit entirely (μ near zero or positive, slow or no recovery).

The Lyapunov exponent of a written argument is the rate at which it returns to its central conclusion after a perturbation. A well-structured Methods section has a high negative μ: describe a flaw in the protocol, and the sentence immediately following acknowledges it and re-establishes the main trajectory. A poorly structured Discussion has a low negative μ: introduce a limitation, and the paper never quite returns to a coherent interpretation.

The stability radius ε₀ = 1/3 translates to academic writing as follows: a claim can survive perturbations of up to 1/3 its evidential support before it leaves the basin of attraction. A claim supported by five pieces of evidence can withstand having one of them challenged — 1/5 = 0.2 < 1/3. A claim supported by only two pieces of evidence cannot withstand one being removed — 1/2 > 1/3. This is why review articles require multiple independent lines of evidence. It is not conservatism. It is the stability radius.

Theorem μ.2 — The Stability Radius of Arguments
Let C be a central claim supported by n independent lines of evidence {e₁, …, eₙ}. Define the evidential perturbation radius as 1/n (the fraction removed by losing any single piece of evidence). Then C is stable against single-evidence failure if and only if 1/n < ε₀ = 1/3 — that is, n ≥ 4 independent supporting results.
Remark: This formalizes the informal peer-review heuristic that a single result does not constitute a finding. Three independent replications (n = 3) gives 1/3 = ε₀ — the boundary. Four independent lines (n = 4) gives 1/4 < ε₀ — stable. The number is not arbitrary; it is the stability radius of the dm³ system applied to evidential structure. □

Exercises

μ.1 — The exponential decay e^{−2t} means that after time t = ln(10)/2 ≈ 1.15 units, a perturbation has shrunk to 10% of its original size. Write a 150-word paragraph about a claim in your field that has a "fast recovery" structure — one that can absorb a challenge and return to its conclusion within two sentences. What makes the recovery fast? What are its load-bearing sentences?

μ.2 — Identify a claim in your current paper draft that has a Lyapunov exponent near zero: one that, once challenged, never quite returns to a definite conclusion. Rewrite the paragraph so that every deviation from the central claim is followed within one sentence by a return. Measure the before and after: how many sentences does the return take in each version?

μ.3 — Count the independent lines of evidence supporting your main conclusion. If n < 4, your claim has 1/n > ε₀ = 1/3: it is outside the stability radius. Write one sentence naming an additional experiment or observation that would bring n to 4. This is not a limitation — it is the next result to generate.

μ.4 — The relation τ + μ = 0 says: what you gain in stability you give up in memory of origin. A paper that is fully stable — that can survive any reasonable objection — has, by definition, suppressed all dependence on the particular experiments that generated it. At what point does a paper become so stable (so theory-laden) that the original data is no longer the primary support? Write two paragraphs: one arguing that full stability is the goal of all science, one arguing that it is a failure mode.

Lyapunov Stability — Prompts for Research Writing
Use these prompts to audit the stability of your claims. A claim that cannot return to its trajectory after a challenge has a positive Lyapunov exponent — it does not belong in the conclusion.
Prompt μ.1 · Stability Audit
The Recovery Test
Here is my main conclusion: [paste your conclusion sentence]. Now here is the strongest objection a reviewer might make: [paste the objection or let me suggest one]. After the objection, does my Discussion section return to the original conclusion, or does it drift to a weaker or different claim? If it drifts, identify the sentence where the drift begins and rewrite it so that the conclusion is re-established within two sentences. The goal is μ = −2: fast, exponential return to the central claim after perturbation.
Prompt μ.2 · Evidential Stability Radius
Counting Independent Lines
My central claim is: [paste claim]. My supporting evidence includes: [list each piece of evidence on a separate line]. For each piece of evidence: (1) is it truly independent of the others, or does it share a methodological assumption? (2) if this single piece were removed or disproved, would the claim survive? Count only truly independent lines. If I have fewer than 4 independent lines (ε₀ = 1/3 boundary), suggest one additional experiment or observation that would increase the evidential count and bring the claim into the stability radius.
Prompt μ.3 · The Mirror Relation
Stability vs. Memory of Origin
The dm³ system has τ + μ = 0: what it gains in stability it loses in memory of where it started. Apply this to my paper. My most stable claim — the one I am most confident in — is: [paste it]. Now: to what degree does this claim still depend on the specific data I collected, as opposed to the theoretical framework I used? If the framework alone would have predicted the same conclusion, is the data still necessary? Write one paragraph arguing that the data is essential. Write one paragraph arguing that the theory has already absorbed it. Which paragraph is more honest about what my paper actually contributes?
